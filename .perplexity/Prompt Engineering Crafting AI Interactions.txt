The geometry and structure of a quantum dot-based light-emitting diode (QD-LED) consists of a highly specialized stack of nanoscale layers, each serving a distinct function in the transport of charges and the generation of light
.
The specific layers, from the base to the top contact, include:
Substrate Layer: Typically made of glass with a thickness of 200 nm, this layer provides structural and mechanical support while minimizing light loss
.
Anode Layer: Composed of indium tin oxide (ITO) with a thickness of 50 nm. It features high transparency to allow for light transmission and facilitates hole injection into the LED structure
.
Hole Injection Layer (HIL): Made of PEDOT:PSS at a thickness of 40 nm, this layer improves the transfer of holes to the active layers and reduces electrical resistance
.
Hole Transport Layer (HTL): Consisting of TPD at a thickness of 50 nm, it prevents electron leakage toward the anode and enhances the overall efficiency of hole injection
.
Emission Layer (EML): This is the active core of the device, composed of indium phosphide quantum dots (InP QDs) in a 20 nm thick layer. These QDs typically have diameters ranging from 2 nm to 10 nm, and their primary role is to produce light through electron-hole recombination
.
Electron Transport Layer (ETL) / Plasmonic Layer: Made of ZnO with a thickness of 30 nm, this layer blocks hole transport to the cathode, enhances electron mobility, and enables localized surface plasmon enhancement
.
Plasmonic Nanoparticles: Integrated within or near the ETL, these are often composed of noble metals like gold (Au) with a diameter of 14 nm. They generate localized electromagnetic fields that interact with the QDs to boost light intensity and increase external quantum efficiency (EQE)
.
Cathode (Electron Injection Layer): Made of aluminum (Al) with a thickness of 200 nm, this layer establishes electrical contact and is responsible for electron injection
.
